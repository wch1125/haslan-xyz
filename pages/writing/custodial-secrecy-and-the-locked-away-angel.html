Custodial Secrecy and the Locked-Away Angel

Sketch 18 December 2025

Abstract: This entry sketches a custodial theory of secrecy: a small group treats certain information as too destabilizing for open circulation and assumes indefinite stewardship over access. The model does not require coordinated malice; it requires asymmetry, incentives, and an apparatus that defaults to classification. A satirical parable—plasmoids mistaken for angels—illustrates the moral hazard: when salvation is treated as a containment problem, scarcity becomes self-inflicted.

Orientation

Secrecy is often narrated as either (a) protection from panic or (b) protection of power. Both frames are incomplete. A more stable explanation is custodial: certain institutions adopt a standing presumption that some knowledge cannot be safely distributed and therefore must be governed through permanent access control.“Custodial” here is descriptive, not exculpatory. It names a posture: stewardship, risk aversion, and long-duration control of interfaces.

This entry treats “anomalous phenomena” as a placeholder category. No factual claims about non-human intelligence are required. The question is institutional: what happens when an organization believes it has encountered something that is (i) uncertain, (ii) high-impact, and (iii) difficult to communicate without triggering misuse?

Custodial Secrecy

Custodial Secrecy emerges when the costs of disclosure are imagined as unbounded and the costs of containment are treated as bounded. In that arithmetic, almost any uncertainty can be converted into a reason to wait.

  • Asymmetry: A small group sees more raw signal than outsiders (or believes it does).
  • Incentives: Career, reputation, and mission alignment favor “no surprises.”
  • Path dependence: Once a category is classified, it becomes harder to unclassify than to continue classifying.
  • Interface control: Access becomes the product. Interpretation becomes the monopoly.

Over time, custodial logic stops being an emergency measure and becomes an identity: not “we are hiding X,” but “we are the people qualified to hold X.”This is how secrecy acquires a priesthood-like shape without requiring religion. The structure is the same: controlled access, sanctioned interpretation, and the promise of safety.

Interpretive Capture

Custodial regimes do not only restrict facts; they restrict meaning. The easiest way to govern an unknown is to govern the narratives permitted to form around it. This is Interpretive Capture.

In practice, capture often looks banal:

  • Redactions that remove the parameters required for independent analysis.
  • Standardized language that converts irregular observations into approved categories.
  • Briefings that emphasize uncertainty as a reason to delay rather than a reason to widen inquiry.

This is not necessarily deception. It is frequently a conflict between two aims: preserving operational security and preserving an Epistemic Commons. The custodial move prioritizes the former and treats the latter as optional.

Disclosure Gradients

Most controversies about “disclosure” are actually controversies about the correct gradient: what is revealed, to whom, under what constraints, with what supporting evidence, and with what institutional accountability.“All or nothing” is emotionally satisfying but operationally rare. Most real-world disclosures are partial, staged, and optimized for damage control.

A custodial apparatus prefers gradients that preserve Interface Risk management. The public prefers gradients that preserve legitimacy and trust. The clash is predictable:

  • Custodians: “We need control before we can talk.”
  • Public: “We need visibility before we can trust.”

When the gradient is mismatched, suspicion expands to fill the void. Secrecy becomes evidence of intent rather than a technique of governance.

The Plasmoid Parable

Consider a satirical premise: luminous plasmoids drift in the upper atmosphere and are routinely mistaken for angels. Each plasmoid, if studied, could power an entire continent for a decade. The tragedy is not scarcity; the tragedy is access control.

In the parable, a committee classifies the phenomenon as an existential hazard. Their reasons vary—national security, reputational risk, fear of misuse—but the outcome is stable: the “angels” are quarantined as if salvation were contraband.Satire works here because it isolates a moral hazard: the institution tasked with protection can become the primary source of harm when protection hardens into indefinite withholding.

The irony is sharp: as the global energy shortage intensifies, the symbol of relief in religious imagination literally descends from heaven, yet remains unavailable because a custodial class has mapped its own governance anxieties onto it. The object could be a battery; it becomes a sacrament. The result is preventable suffering.

Implications

The custodial model predicts several recurring features of classified-anomaly discourse:

  • Slow disclosure: not because nothing is known, but because knowledge is treated as a controlled interface.
  • Narrative volatility: gaps in evidence invite maximal stories, both utopian and apocalyptic.
  • Legitimacy drift: institutions lose credibility precisely when they claim to act for stability.

The corrective is not naive transparency. It is governance: auditable processes, bounded secrecy, and public-facing epistemic hygiene. The goal is to prevent Custodial Overhang—the condition where yesterday’s caution becomes today’s unnecessary enclosure.

Speculative extensions

If a program believed it was engaging with an intelligence outside ordinary frameworks, custodial logic would intensify: the fear would not be the phenomenon itself, but the downstream behavior of humans once access becomes competitive. In that regime, secrecy becomes a form of governance-by-denial: not “we solved it,” but “we prevent you from touching it.”

This extension is a narrative affordance rather than a factual claim. Its usefulness is explanatory: it models why an institution might treat access as the most dangerous variable.
