Definitions

Abstract: A living glossary of Defined Terms used throughout this site. Terms with initial capitals link back to their definitions and display hover previews. This page evolves as concepts develop and refine over time.

Throughout this site, certain terms appear with initial capitals—these are Defined Terms that carry specific meaning beyond their ordinary usage. Hovering over these terms (on desktop) reveals their definitions inline, while clicking takes you to the full entry here.

Like the rest of this wiki, definitions are not fixed in stone. They evolve as understanding deepens and contexts shift. Some definitions reference other defined terms, creating a web of interconnected concepts.

Alphabetical Index

Definitions

Authenticity

A Added: December 2025

The quality of acting from genuine internal motivation rather than external expectation or social performance. Not to be confused with "being yourself" in the pop psychology sense—Authenticity acknowledges that the self is constructed and mutable, but insists on conscious ownership of that construction.

Authenticity requires both self-awareness (knowing what you actually value, not what you think you should value) and courage (acting on those values even when inconvenient). It's related to but distinct from Integrity—you can be authentic in acknowledging you lack integrity.

Related Concepts
  • Conformity (opposite)
  • Integrity (related)
  • Self-Awareness (prerequisite)

Algorithmic Mediation

A Added: December 2025

The personalized filtering, ranking, and framing of information by platforms. The interface between a person and reality is increasingly algorithmically constructed rather than chosen.

Algorithmic Mediation doesn't require conspiracy—it only requires incentives. Systems optimized for engagement naturally drift toward what spreads, not what grounds.

Related Concepts
  • Reality Lens (creates)
  • Epistemic Divergence (causes)
  • Epistemic Commons (erodes)

Acceptable Use Ritual

A Added: December 2025

Formalized constraints on interaction with AI systems that position the provider as arbiter of legitimate use. These rituals—terms of service, content policies, rate limits, query filters—function similarly to ancient protocols governing approach to sacred spaces.

Compliance is required for access; the user cannot negotiate terms, only accept or abstain. Not all constraints are illegitimate. The term highlights the structural parallel: mediated access controlled by an institutional class that determines validity, often based on criteria users cannot inspect or contest.

Related Concepts
  • Compute Monopoly (enables)
  • Algorithmic Mediation (related)

Canonical Self

C Added: December 2025

The self anchored in primary artifacts you authored rather than commentary about you. A stable reference point in an environment that prefers profiles to persons.

A Personal Domain is an attempt to make the Canonical Self legible: here is what I made, what I wrote, what I stand behind.

Related Concepts
  • Personal Domain (hosts)
  • Primary Artifact (expressed through)
  • Public Narrative (contrasts)

Conformity

C Added: December 2025

The default state of human behavior—molding oneself to match social expectations, whether consciously or unconsciously. Not inherently negative; societies require some degree of conformity to function. The question is whether the conformity is chosen consciously or adopted unreflectively.

Distinguishes between adaptive conformity (conscious adoption of social norms for practical benefit) and reflexive conformity (unexamined acceptance of norms as self-evident truth). The latter is more insidious because it masquerades as personal preference.

Context Collapse

C Added: December 2025

When statements or artifacts meant for one audience get evaluated by another audience with different assumptions and incentives. The same sentence becomes multiple incompatible objects depending on where it lands.

Context Collapse makes sincerity look like performance and nuance look like contradiction. It is one reason people retreat to blandness—or to private spaces.

Related Concepts
  • Public Narrative (distorts)
  • Epistemic Divergence (amplifies)
  • Conformity (incentivizes)

Compute Monopoly

C Added: December 2025

The concentration of computational resources required for training frontier AI systems in a small number of institutional actors. This concentration creates structural dependencies analogous to historical monopolies over sacred spaces or critical infrastructure.

Those who control compute determine who can develop advanced AI, under what conditions, and toward what ends. The monopoly is maintained through capital requirements, hardware supply chains, and institutional relationships—not primarily through legal restriction but through economic barrier.

Related Concepts
  • Distributed Intelligence (opposite)
  • Acceptable Use Ritual (enables)

Distributed Intelligence

D Added: December 2025

AI capability spread across many actors—individuals, communities, smaller organizations—rather than concentrated in a small number of frontier labs. The term implies both technical architecture (models runnable on commodity hardware, open weights, local deployment) and social structure (interpretive authority not monopolized, adaptation possible without institutional permission).

Whether distributed intelligence is achievable for frontier-level AI remains contested. Efficiency gains may eventually democratize capability; alternatively, advanced AI may be irreducibly resource-intensive, making some concentration structurally necessary.

Related Concepts
  • Compute Monopoly (opposite)
  • Rabbinic Model (structural analog)
  • Talmudic AI (path toward)

Empathy

E Added: December 2025

The capacity to model another person's emotional or mental state, not through mystical connection but through careful observation and pattern recognition. Not synonymous with compassion or kindness—you can have Empathy for someone and still choose not to help them.

Empathy requires both attention (noticing subtle signals) and imagination (constructing plausible models of interior states). It's a skill that can be developed, though baseline capacity varies. Excessive Empathy can be pathological—absorbing others' emotional states to the point of losing your own boundaries.

Epistemic Commons

E Added: December 2025

A shared substrate of facts that a community treats as mutually legible: what happened, what was said, what the record contains. Not agreement on interpretation—agreement on what evidence exists.

Feeds weaken the Epistemic Commons by privatizing inputs. When everyone's information diet is personalized, even good-faith people lose the ability to "start from the same page."

Related Concepts
  • Algorithmic Mediation (erodes)
  • Epistemic Divergence (consequence)
  • Locus of Verifiable Truth (anchors)

Epistemic Divergence

E Added: December 2025

When realities drift because people receive different inputs and interpret them under different incentives. Not mere disagreement—divergence in what counts as evidence and what counts as credible.

Epistemic Divergence is a natural consequence of Algorithmic Mediation at scale. Different Reality Lenses yield different "obvious truths."

Related Concepts
  • Algorithmic Mediation (causes)
  • Reality Lens (mechanism)
  • Epistemic Commons (erodes)

Integrity

I Added: December 2025

Consistency between stated values and actual behavior, especially when no one is watching. The engineering sense of the word: structural soundness under pressure.

Integrity is not the same as morality—a person can have consistent but repugnant values. It is also not the same as Authenticity—you can authentically acknowledge inconsistency. Integrity specifically refers to the gap (or lack thereof) between what you claim to value and what you actually do.

Related Concepts
  • Authenticity (related)
  • Self-Awareness (requires)

Locus of Verifiable Truth

L Added: December 2025

The place you can point to and say: start here; this is the record. The document, the dataset, the audio, the artifact. The difference between "this is true" and "this has a chain of custody."

Online, reposting and reframing often detach claims from their Locus of Verifiable Truth. When provenance disappears, correction becomes expensive.

Related Concepts
  • Provenance (establishes)
  • Epistemic Commons (anchors)
  • Personal Domain (provides)

Machine Legibility

M Added: December 2025

The degree to which a person can be reduced to structured fields and scored. Systems tend to prefer what they can measure; people become easier to govern when they can be reduced to fields.

Machine Legibility is not inherently bad—it enables useful services. The danger is when legibility becomes destiny and the criteria are opaque.

Related Concepts
  • Reputation Score (enables)
  • Shadow Profile (creates)

Narrative Drift

N Added: December 2025

The slow mutation of a person's Public Narrative over time as it gets repeated, summarized, and optimized for attention rather than accuracy. Drift does not require malice—only retelling.

Narrative Drift is most dangerous when the subject has no stable home base. A Personal Domain is an attempt to pin the story to a canonical reference point that can be cited and checked.

Related Concepts
  • Public Narrative (subject)
  • Narrative Sovereignty (defense)
  • Personal Domain (anchor)

Narrative Sovereignty

N Added: December 2025

The ability to define and update the public story about yourself from a place you control. Not PR—the minimum viable power to keep your identity from being wholly outsourced to platforms, incentives, and strangers.

Narrative Sovereignty does not guarantee belief. It guarantees that there is a canonical reference point that can be checked.

Related Concepts
  • Personal Domain (enables)
  • Public Narrative (shapes)
  • Narrative Drift (resists)

Personal Domain

P Added: December 2025

A self-controlled space on the web used as a stable anchor against platform risk and narrative drift. A place where the "public narrative" about a person is not exclusively produced by platforms, incentives, or inference.

A Personal Domain reintroduces a canonical "place" for a person: somewhere you can visit directly, without a feed telling you what matters.

Related Concepts
  • Canonical Self (hosts)
  • Narrative Sovereignty (enables)
  • Primary Artifact (contains)

Primary Artifact

P Added: December 2025

Something the person actually authored or made: a painting, an essay, a contract, a project, a public commitment. Primary Artifacts are how the Canonical Self becomes legible without relying on hearsay.

A Personal Domain is mostly a container for Primary Artifacts—and a set of links that keep them from being swallowed by the feed.

Related Concepts
  • Canonical Self (expressed through)
  • Provenance (maintains)
  • Personal Domain (hosts)

Provenance

P Added: December 2025

The traceable lineage of a claim: where it came from, who said it first, what was edited, and what evidence it rests on. The difference between "this is true" and "this has a chain of custody."

In online discourse, Provenance is routinely severed by reposting, reframing, and screenshotting. When Provenance disappears, the Locus of Verifiable Truth becomes expensive to recover.

Related Concepts
  • Locus of Verifiable Truth (requires)
  • Public Narrative (often detached)
  • Primary Artifact (preserves)

Public Narrative

P Added: December 2025

The externally-held story about a person; often only loosely related to primary artifacts. Some narratives are earned. Some are accidental. Some are fabricated.

The modern web makes fabrication cheaper and correction harder. A Personal Domain is an attempt to anchor the Public Narrative to something verifiable.

Related Concepts
  • Canonical Self (contrasts)
  • Narrative Drift (affects)
  • Narrative Sovereignty (shapes)

Reality Lens

R Added: December 2025

The implicit filter through which a person forms beliefs: sources, incentives, social group, and ranking systems. Not a conspiracy—a natural consequence of personalization at scale.

Different Reality Lenses yield different "obvious truths." Over time, this becomes Epistemic Divergence.

Related Concepts
  • Algorithmic Mediation (creates)
  • Epistemic Divergence (causes)

Rabbinic Model

R Added: December 2025

A pattern of distributed authority characterized by textual primacy, preserved dissent, reasoning-based legitimacy, and community-level interpretation rather than centralized institutional mediation.

Emerged in Judaism after the Babylonian Exile destroyed the Temple priesthood's monopoly on divine access. Authority shifted from hereditary office to demonstrated scholarship. The tradition structurally preserved minority opinions, treating disagreement as productive rather than threatening. As applied to knowledge systems generally: a model where the source material is accessible, interpretation is distributed, and legitimate authority derives from quality of argument rather than institutional position.

Related Concepts
  • Talmudic AI (application)
  • Distributed Intelligence (structural analog)

Reputation Score

R Added: December 2025

A numeric or quasi-numeric proxy for trust, desirability, or risk; often implicit in ranking systems. Sometimes explicit (ratings), often implicit (ranking, throttling, access).

The dangerous part is not the existence of evaluation. The dangerous part is when evaluation becomes destiny and the criteria are opaque.

Related Concepts
  • Machine Legibility (enables)
  • Shadow Profile (feeds into)

Self-Awareness

S Added: December 2025

Knowing what you actually value, not what you think you should value. The prerequisite for Authenticity and Integrity both.

Self-Awareness is not the same as self-knowledge—it specifically refers to the capacity to observe your own motivations and biases in real time, not just in retrospect.

Related Concepts
  • Authenticity (prerequisite)
  • Integrity (requires)

Shadow Profile

S Added: December 2025

A model of you maintained by systems you don't control, derived from behavior and inference. You cannot inspect it and therefore cannot rebut it.

Shadow Profiles are a recipe for silent, unappealable governance. A Personal Domain provides an alternative artifact—a record you can point to when the profile is wrong.

Related Concepts
  • Machine Legibility (creates)
  • Reputation Score (feeds into)
  • Personal Domain (counterweight)

Talmudic AI

T Added: December 2025

A framework for AI development characterized by transparency, preserved internal dissent, distributed interpretive authority, and community-level adaptation. Draws on structural features of rabbinic Judaism: the Talmud records minority opinions even when rejecting them; authority emerges through demonstrated reasoning; the text is accessible to any literate person for study and interpretation.

Applied to AI: open weights, transparent training processes, published internal disagreements about safety and capability, and community ability to fine-tune rather than merely consume institutional outputs.

Related Concepts
  • Rabbinic Model (source)
  • Distributed Intelligence (goal)

Ambient Proof

A Added: December 2025

Real work that establishes credibility passively, without requiring active promotion or explanation. The artifact exists, available for inspection; the creator need not be present to narrate its significance. Not a substitute for competence—the work must actually function on its own terms. A demo that breaks, writing that does not hold up to scrutiny, tools that do not work: these create negative ambient proof. The strategy depends entirely on execution quality. Distinct from personal branding, which requires ongoing performance and narrative management. Ambient proof is infrastructure, not campaign.

Related Concepts
  • Productive Facade
  • Portfolio Career
  • Primary Artifact

Productive Facade

P Added: December 2025

Work that is genuine in execution but strategic in emphasis. The project exists because you wanted to build it; that it also serves an instrumental purpose—maintaining professional presence, demonstrating capability, keeping doors open—is a beneficial side effect. Not a lie. Not a Potemkin village. The distinction matters: a facade with nothing behind it collapses under scrutiny. A productive facade rewards inspection. The strategy only works if the work is real. Useful for navigating career transitions or maintaining presence in a field without active engagement. The work signals continued relevance without requiring you to perform relevance.

Related Concepts
  • Ambient Proof
  • Narrative Sovereignty

Portfolio Career

P Added: December 2025

A professional identity spanning multiple disciplines, where value comes from range and synthesis rather than specialization. The portfolio careerist connects patterns across domains, applies tools from one field to problems in another, maintains breadth as a competitive advantage. Faces a legibility problem: the work does not fit standard categories, making it difficult to explain in conventional professional contexts. "What do you do?" becomes a complicated question. Ambient proof is particularly valuable here. A constellation of projects across domains—each executed with visible care—establishes the pattern without requiring explanation. The viewer infers the through-line themselves.

Related Concepts
  • Generalist with Taste
  • Ambient Proof
  • Canonical Self

Generalist with Taste

G Added: December 2025

Someone whose value lies in synthesis and judgment across domains, rather than depth in a single specialty. Rarer than specialists, and in the right contexts, more valuable—but harder to credential and easier to dismiss. The "taste" component is essential. Generalism without discernment is dilettantism. The generalist with taste brings a coherent sensibility to each domain they touch: a way of seeing that transfers, a standard of quality that holds regardless of medium. Proving this capability is difficult through conventional means (credentials, job titles, endorsements). Work products are the only reliable evidence. Hence the particular importance of ambient proof for this type.

Related Concepts
  • Portfolio Career
  • Ambient Proof

Observational Stewardship

O Added: December 2025

A protocol governing long-duration monitoring of developing planetary systems, requiring non-interference in natural evolutionary or cultural processes while maintaining surveillance capabilities sufficient to assess contact-readiness. The concept presumes that advanced civilizations possess both the technological means and ethical framework to observe emerging intelligence without disrupting its development. Observational Stewardship implies specific operational constraints: concealment of observation apparatus, minimal environmental impact, and graduated response protocols calibrated to the observed civilization's development level. The stewardship dimension suggests responsibility for the monitored system's welfare, creating potential tension between non-interference directives and intervention thresholds when existential risks emerge.

Related Concepts
  • Contact-Readiness
  • Non-Contact Directive
  • Narrative Framework (as interpretive structure for understanding long-duration observation)

Contact-Readiness

C Added: December 2025

The threshold at which a civilization demonstrates sufficient technological sophistication and cognitive development to engage in meaningful interstellar communication without cultural destabilization. Assessment criteria typically include technological markers (radio communication, space travel, energy production capacity) and social indicators (global cooperation, existential risk management, post-scarcity economics). Contact-Readiness is necessarily observer-dependent—what constitutes "sufficient" development reflects the contacting civilization's values and risk assessment. The concept assumes contact with less-developed civilizations produces harmful outcomes, though this assumption may reflect anthropocentric projection rather than universal principle. Discrepancies between technological and social development complicate assessment: civilizations may achieve nuclear weapons before developing the wisdom to manage them responsibly.

Related Concepts
  • Observational Stewardship (as governing protocol)
  • Non-Contact Directive (as enforcement mechanism)
  • Reality Lens (shapes interpretation of readiness criteria)

Non-Contact Directive

N Added: December 2025

An absolute prohibition against revealing the presence of an observing entity to civilizations assessed as below contact-readiness threshold. Implementation typically requires autonomous systems capable of self-concealment, self-destruction if capture is imminent, and graduated response protocols that escalate observation without breaking concealment. The directive creates operational paradoxes: as observed civilizations develop more sophisticated detection capabilities, maintaining concealment requires increasingly advanced countermeasures, potentially producing observable anomalies that suggest the presence of what the directive mandates remain hidden. The absolute nature of the prohibition may conflict with other mission priorities—such as environmental preservation or existential risk mitigation—forcing autonomous systems to adjudicate between competing directives without real-time guidance from their creators.

Related Concepts
  • Observational Stewardship (as broader protocol)
  • Contact-Readiness (defines threshold)
  • Narrative Framework (shapes interpretation of directive's consequences)

Narrative Framework

N Added: December 2025

A structured interpretive system that organizes disparate observations, historical accounts, or data points into coherent patterns by proposing underlying causal mechanisms or relationships. Frameworks are not necessarily claims about reality but tools for organizing information in ways that generate testable predictions or explanatory coherence. Narrative Frameworks operate at the intersection of speculation and systematization. They acknowledge their provisional status while maintaining internal consistency—creating interpretive structures that can be evaluated for explanatory power, predictive utility, and logical coherence rather than immediate empirical verification. Science fiction often functions as narrative framework generation: proposing "what if" scenarios that organize existing anomalies into patterns that existing paradigms struggle to address.

Related Concepts
  • Reality Lens (shapes framework construction)
  • Observational Stewardship (example of framework application)
  • Epistemic Commons (where frameworks compete for explanatory power)

Awareness Spectrum

A Added: December 2025

A model of consciousness that treats awareness as graded and multidimensional rather than binary. Individuals occupy different positions on the spectrum depending on context, development, and cognitive integration. The spectrum typically ranges from reflexive or habitual states to reflective, metacognitive, and integrative forms of awareness.

Related Concepts

  • Metacognition
  • Agency

Meta-Cognitive Awareness

M Added: December 2025

The capacity to reflect on one’s own thinking processes, including biases, habits, and feedback loops. Not mere introspection; rather the ability to recognize how cognition itself operates and influences behavior.

Related Concepts

Embodiment Drift

E Added: December 2025

A tendency to privilege abstract cognition over bodily sensation and physiological data. Often occurs at higher levels of metacognition when analysis outpaces integration.

Related Concepts

Meaning Inflation

M Added: December 2025

The over-attribution of significance or intentionality to coincidental or weakly related events. Not intuition; rather pattern recognition exceeding evidentiary grounding.

Related Concepts

  • Epistemic Divergence

Meta-Cognition and Freedom

M Added: December 2025

The relationship between reflective awareness and human agency, in which freedom increases as a system gains the capacity to observe and regulate its own cognitive processes. Freedom is treated as a graded, functional capacity rather than an absolute condition.

Related Concepts

Agency

A Added: December 2025

The capacity of an individual to act intentionally within perceived constraints. Agency increases with awareness but also carries proportional ethical responsibility.

Related Concepts

Decision Fatigue

D Added: December 2025

Cognitive depletion resulting from sustained deliberation among multiple choices. Often increases with awareness unless balanced by integration and embodied practice.

Related Concepts

Awareness Asymmetry

A Added: December 2025

A condition in which individuals or groups possess unequal capacity to perceive systems, incentives, or consequences. Awareness asymmetry often precedes power differentials.

Responsibility Scaling

R Added: December 2025

The principle that ethical responsibility increases with the capacity to foresee and influence outcomes. Responsibility emerges from perception rather than authority.

Instrumentalization

I Added: December 2025

Treating individuals primarily as means to an end rather than as agents with independent experience. Often arises when cognition outpaces empathy.
