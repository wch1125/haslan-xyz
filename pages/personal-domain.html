Personal Domains and Narrative Sovereignty

17 December 2025

Abstract: The web used to feel like a collection of places. Now it often feels like one place with many feeds. When information is ranked and personalized, people inherit different realities—and the public story about a person becomes easy to distort. A personal domain is my attempt at a counterweight: a stable, self-authored record that is not optimized for someone else's incentives.

I don't think the internet is "lying" to us so much as it is mediating us. The default interface to reality is now a feed: filtered, ranked, and tuned to maximize some objective function that is not truth.

This doesn't require a conspiracy. It only requires incentives. Ranking systems inevitably reward what spreads, not what's grounded. People are then surprised when two competent adults form incompatible convictions about the same event, the same idea, or the same person.This is not "everyone is irrational." It's often a perfectly rational response to different inputs and different perceived costs of dissent inside different social graphs.

My response is simple: maintain at least one place on the web that I control—a Personal Domain—and treat it as a canonical record of what I make and what I think.

Feeds create Reality Lenses

When you ask the internet "Who is John Doe?", you are not asking a library. You are asking a ranking system. The answer you receive is conditioned by your prior clicks, your network, your location, your language, your platform, and the platform's incentives. That conditioning becomes a Reality Lens.

Different lenses yield different "obvious truths." Over time, this becomes Epistemic Divergence: not just disagreement, but divergence in what counts as evidence and what counts as credible.

Why this feels new (even though propaganda is old)

Propaganda is old. Personalization at scale is newer. A broadcast channel gives many people the same distortion. A feed gives each person a custom distortion. That makes the shared "public world" harder to recover.

It also makes mistakes harder to correct. When each person's information diet is private and continuously shifting, there is no single front page to rebut.

The Locus of Verifiable Truth is harder to point to

In a healthier information environment, you can gesture toward primary sources: the document, the dataset, the audio, the place. Online, reposting and reframing often detach claims from provenance. The Locus of Verifiable Truth becomes obscured.

This is why "public narrative" becomes unstable. People don't just disagree about interpretations—they disagree about what was even said, done, or meant.

What I mean by "truth" here (and what I do not mean)

I'm not claiming my pages are "the truth." I'm claiming they are the record I can stand behind: authored, maintained, revised transparently, and grounded in primary artifacts where possible.

In other words: a stable reference point. If you want to know what I actually make or think, start with what I wrote and what I built, not what a feed inferred.

Narrative Sovereignty is a defense against outsourcing your identity

Every person acquires a Public Narrative. Some narratives are earned. Some are accidental. Some are fabricated. The modern web makes fabrication cheaper and correction harder.

Narrative Sovereignty is not PR. It is the minimum viable power to keep your identity from being wholly outsourced to platforms, incentives, and strangers.

This is where I find the "early web" model useful: a set of places you can visit directly, without a feed telling you what matters. A personal domain reintroduces a canonical "place" for a person: the Canonical Self.

Judge me by what you see here, not by whatever story is assembled elsewhere.This is not a demand for belief. It's an invitation to examine primary artifacts. Skepticism is fine; the point is to relocate the starting point.

Machine Legibility and invisible scoring

Systems tend to prefer what they can measure. People become easier to govern when they can be reduced to fields: risk, reliability, desirability, compliance. That reduction is Machine Legibility.

Once legible, a person can be assigned a Reputation Score—sometimes explicit (ratings), often implicit (ranking, throttling, access). The dangerous part is not the existence of evaluation. The dangerous part is when evaluation becomes destiny and the criteria are opaque.

Meanwhile, platforms maintain Shadow Profiles that you cannot inspect and therefore cannot rebut. That is a recipe for silent, unappealable governance.

Why a personal domain helps (even if it doesn't "solve" anything)

A personal domain doesn't eliminate scoring. It reduces dependency. It provides a stable alternative artifact: a record you can point to when the score is wrong, when the platform vanishes, or when someone attempts to rewrite you.

It also disciplines the author. Maintaining a canonical record encourages Integrity because incoherence becomes visible over time.

What I'm doing in practice

  • Own the address: a domain I control.
  • Own the artifacts: writing, paintings, projects, and definitions in plain files.
  • Prefer primary sources: link outward, but keep the canonical record local.
  • Keep it readable: short pages, cross-links, and margin notes.
  • Accept evolution: evergreen revision rather than performance for a timeline.

None of this requires fame to matter. It matters because it is a way to remain a person in an environment that prefers profiles.
